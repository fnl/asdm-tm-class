'''Burton Hill "Burt" Mustin''' (February 8, 1884<ref name="joseph" /><ref>{{cite book|last=Koykka|first=Arthur S. |title=Project Remember: ANational Index Of Gravesites Of Notable Americans|year=1986|publisher=Reference Publications|isbn=0-917-25622-0|page=164}}</ref>&nbsp;– January 28, 1977) was an American [[character actor]].<ref name="WVobit">Obituary ''[[Variety Obituaries|Variety]]'', February 2, 1977, page 94.</ref> Over the course of his career, Mustin appeared in over 150 film and television productions. He also worked in radio and appeared in stage productions.

Mustin began his professional acting career at the age of 67 after director [[William Wyler]] cast him in the 1951 film noir ''[[Detective Story (1951 film)|Detective Story]]''. Known for his dependability and versatility, Mustin would go on to establish a career as a well-known character actor and worked extensively in film and television from the 1950s to the 1970s.<ref name=bocaraton>{{cite news|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=BhIQAAAAIBAJ&sjid=04wDAAAAIBAJ&pg=6697,5238526&dq=burt+mustin+johnny+carson&hl=en|title=Burt Mustin|date=April 7, 1975|work=Boca Raton News|accessdate=December 12, 2012}}</ref>

In January 1977, Mustin died in [[Glendale, California]] at the age of 92.

==Early life==
Mustin was born in [[Pittsburgh]] to William I. and Sadie (Dorrington) Mustin. His father worked as a [[stockbroker]]. Mustin graduated from [[Widener University|Pennsylvania Military College]] (renamed Widener University in 1972) in 1903 with a degree in [[civil engineering]]. He also played goaltender for their ice hockey team in 1902.<ref>{{cite web|last=Widener University|title=Burt Mustin|url=http://digitalwolfgram.widener.edu/cdm/singleitem/collection/p16069coll1/id/41/rec/17|publisher=Widener University|accessdate=June 16, 2013}}</ref>  He worked as an engineer but later decided to go into sales. In 1916, Mustin began working as an automobile salesman selling [[Oakland (automobile)|Oakland Sensible Six]]es. He later began selling the luxury [[Franklin (automobile)|Franklin]]s. After the Franklin company went out of business, Mustin sold [[Mercury (automobile)|Mercury]]s and [[Lincoln Motor Company|Lincolns]] until the car industry began to suffer due to [[World War I]]. He then worked as a fiscal agent for the [[Better Business Bureau]] and the Chamber of Commerce.<ref name="toledo">{{cite news|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=_-gpAAAAIBAJ&sjid=zQEEAAAAIBAJ&pg=7141,5460374&dq=burt+mustin+married&hl=en|title=Mustin, Life Begins At 87|last=Smith|first=Cecil|date=June 5, 1971|work=Toledo Blade|accessdate=December 12, 2012}}</ref><ref name=postgazette>{{cite news|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=eM8nAAAAIBAJ&sjid=2GwDAAAAIBAJ&pg=5788,663628&dq=burt+mustin+actor&hl=en|title=Burt Mustin Active, But Hollywood Isn't|date=March 5, 1970|work=Pittsburgh Post-Gazette|accessdate=December 12, 2012}}</ref>

Before he began a professional career in show business, Mustin did amateur acting and performing.  In 1921, he became the first announcer for a variety show broadcast on Pittsburgh's then newly established [[KDKA (AM)|KDKA]] radio station. He appeared in productions in the Pittsburgh Savoyards (a [[Gilbert and Sullivan]] troupe) and the [[Pittsburgh Opera]]. He was also a member of the [[Barbershop Harmony Society]].<ref name=bocaraton /><ref name="toledo" />

After retiring, Mustin moved to [[Tucson, Arizona]]. While living in Tucson, director [[William Wyler]] saw him in a stage production of ''[[Detective Story (play)|Detective Story]]''. Wyler told Mustin to look him up if he decided to pursue a screen career. Mustin contacted Wyler who cast him in the 1951 film version of ''[[Detective Story (1951 film)|Detective Story]]''. After appearing in the film, Mustin's acting career took off and he began landing roles in films and television series. He later moved to [[Los Angeles]].<ref name="joseph">{{cite news|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=mxZhAAAAIBAJ&sjid=0lMNAAAAIBAJ&pg=2800,5751007&dq=burt+mustin+actor&hl=en|title=Hollywood actor Mustin claimed by death at 92|date=January 29, 1977|work=St. Joseph News-Press|page=2A|accessdate=December 12, 2012}}</ref><ref name=postgazette />

==Career==

===Television===

====1950s and 1960s====
Mustin made his television debut in 1951 with a role in the [[Western (genre)|Western]] series ''[[The Adventures of Kit Carson]]''. Throughout the rest of the 1950s, he made guest appearances on ''[[Leave It to Beaver]]'', ''[[The Abbott and Costello Show]]'', ''[[The Loretta Young Show]]'', ''[[Cavalcade of America]]'', ''[[The Public Defender (TV series)|The Public Defender]]'', ''[[Treasury Men in Action]]'', ''[[The Lone Ranger (TV series)|The Lone Ranger]]'', ''[[Fireside Theater]]'', ''[[Tales of the Texas Rangers]]'', ''[[Mackenzie's Raiders]]'', ''[[Lux Video Theatre]]'', ''[[Studio 57]]'',  ''[[Dragnet (series)|Dragnet]]'', ''[[Our Miss Brooks#Television|Our Miss Brooks]]'', ''[[It's a Great Life]]'', ''[[The Gale Storm Show]]'', ''[[General Electric Theater]]'', ''[[Peter Gunn]]'', and ''[[The Texan (TV series)|The Texan]]'' among many others. Mustin also starred in the TV series pilot episode of ''[[The Lone Wolf (TV series)|The Lone Wolf]]'' starring [[Louis Hayward]] in 1954.

In 1960, Mustin guest starred on ''[[The Twilight Zone (1959 TV series)|The Twilight Zone]]'' in the episode "[[The Night of the Meek]]" alongside [[Art Carney]]. He also appeared in a second episode of the series, "[[Kick the Can]]" in 1962. In 1964, Mustin had an uncredited role in ''[[The Outer Limits (1963 TV series)|The Outer Limits]]'' episode "[[The Guests (The Outer Limits)|The Guests]]".

During the 1960s, Mustin also made multiple appearances on ''[[Bonanza]]'', ''[[Gunsmoke]]'', ''[[Ichabod and Me]]'', ''[[The Many Loves of Dobie Gillis]]'', ''[[Dragnet (series)|Dragnet]]'', ''[[Alfred Hitchcock Presents|The Alfred Hitchcock Hour]]'', ''[[Get Smart]]'', ''[[The New Phil Silvers Show]]'', ''[[The Beverly Hillbillies]]'', ''[[Dr. Kildare#Television|Dr. Kildare]]'', ''[[The Jack Benny Program]]'', ''[[Ben Casey]]'', ''[[The Monkees]]'', ''[[The Virginian (TV series)|The Virginian]]'', ''[[Cimarron Strip]]'', ''[[My Three Sons]]'', ''[[Batman (TV series)|Batman]]''(episode 48), and ''[[Bewitched]]''. In 1969, he co-starred in the television film ''[[The Over-the-Hill Gang]]''. Mustin also appeared in the sequel film ''[[The Over-the-Hill Gang Rides Again]]'' the following year.

In addition to guest starring roles, Mustin also had recurring roles on several television shows during the 1950s and 1960s. In 1955, he played the role of "Foley" in ''[[The Great Gildersleeve#Television|The Great Gildersleeve]]''. From 1957 to 1958, he appeared as Mr. Finley on ''[[Date with the Angels]]''. In 1957, he made his first appearance as "Gus the Fireman" on ''[[Leave It to Beaver]]''. Mustin would continue in the role until 1962 making a total of 15 appearances on the show. In 1960, he made his first guest appearance on ''[[The Andy Griffith Show]]'' as Jud Fletcher. He appeared in the role until 1966. He also portrayed "Old Uncle Joe" on two episodes of ''[[The Lucy Show]]'' in 1967. The following year, Mustin guest starred as "Grandpa Jenson" on ''[[Petticoat Junction]]'' in three episodes.

====1970s====
During the 1970s, Mustin continued with guest roles on ''[[Love, American Style]]'', ''[[Adam-12]]'', ''[[Emergency!]]'' and ''[[The Brady Bunch]]''.

Known for his quick wit and song-and-dance abilities, Mustin was a frequent closing act on ''[[The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson]]'' during the 1970s.<ref name=bocaraton /> From 1971 to 1976, he appeared in five episodes of ''[[All in the Family]]'' (his first appearance as a night watchman, and an additional four appearances in a recurring role as "Justin Quigley"). In 1971, Mustin co-starred in the [[sketch comedy]] show ''[[The Funny Side]]''. Hosted by [[Gene Kelly]], the series featured an ensemble cast of five married couples that dealt with various issues through comedy sketches and song-and-dance routines. Mustin was cast opposite [[Queenie Smith]] as "the elderly couple". The series debuted on [[NBC]] in September 1971 and was canceled in January 1972.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=ckwqAAAAIBAJ&sjid=lFAEAAAAIBAJ&pg=3476,5270350&dq=burt+mustin+the+funny+side&hl=en|title=Contribution Of Burt And Queenie|last=Kleiner|first=Dick|date=November 28, 1971|work=The Pittsburgh Press|accessdate=December 12, 2012}}</ref>

Mustin guest starred as Jethroe Collins, a relative of a Jesse James victim in the "Bobby's Hero" episode of the Brady Bunch
during the 1972-73 season.

The next year, Mustin co-starred in the television film ''[[Miracle on 34th Street (1973 film)|Miracle on 34th Street]]'', starring [[Sebastian Cabot (actor)|Sebastian Cabot]] and had an uncredited role in the [[Disney]] television film ''[[Now You See Him, Now You Don't]]''. Mustin's last continuing role was on the television series ''[[Phyllis (TV series)|Phyllis]]'', in which he played the suitor, and later husband, of [[Sally "Mother" Dexter]], a role he played until shortly before his death.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=bLszAAAAIBAJ&sjid=vzgHAAAAIBAJ&pg=2198,3652722&dq=burt+mustin+married&hl=en|title=Burt Mustin, 92-year-old actor, is dead|date=January 28, 1977|work=Bangor Daily News|page=33|accessdate=December 12, 2012}}</ref>

===Films===
In addition to his extensive work in television, Mustin also appeared in numerous films. He made his film debut at the age of 67 in ''[[Detective Story (1951 film)|Detective Story]]'', in 1951. Mustin followed with roles in ''[[Talk About a Stranger]]'' (1952), ''[[The Silver Whip]]'' (1953), ''[[Half a Hero]]'' (1953), ''[[She Couldn't Say No (1954 film)|She Couldn't Say No]]'' (1954), ''[[The Desperate Hours (film)|The Desperate Hours]]'' (1955), ''[[Man with the Gun]]'' (1955), ''[[Storm Center]]'' (1956), and ''[[The Sheepman]]'' (1958).

In the 1960s and 1970s, Mustin appeared in ''[[The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn (1960 film)|The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn]]'' (1960), ''[[Hemingway's Adventures of a Young Man]]'' (1962), ''[[Twilight of Honor]]'' (1963), ''[[What a Way to Go!]]'' (1964), ''[[The Misadventures of Merlin Jones]]'' (1964), ''[[Sex and the Single Girl]]'' (1964), ''[[The Cincinnati Kid]]'' (1965), ''[[The Adventures of Bullwhip Griffin]]'' (1967), ''[[Speedway (1968 film)|Speedway]]'' (uncredited) (1968), ''[[The Shakiest Gun in the West]]'' (1968), ''[[The Great Bank Robbery (1969 film)|The Great Bank Robbery]]'' (1969), ''[[Hail, Hero!]]'' (1969), and ''[[Skin Game]]'' (1971). In 1974, Mustin portrayed "Uncle Jeff" in the musical film ''[[Mame (film)|Mame]]'', starring [[Lucille Ball]] and [[Bea Arthur]]. He also had a small role in ''[[Herbie Rides Again]]'', also released in 1974. The next year, he appeared as "Regent Appleby " in ''[[The Strongest Man in the World]]''. His final film role came in 1976 in the [[Western (genre)|Western]] film ''Baker's Hawk'', starring [[Clint Walker]] and [[Burl Ives]].

===Tribute===
In 2000 TVLand created a series of commercials celebrating the often seen but little known by name character actors who regularly appeared in their shows, Mustin being featured in one.

==Personal life==
Mustin was one of the 110 original founders of the Pittsburgh chapter of the [[Lions Club]] which was established in 1921. He served as one of the presidents and remained active in the club for the remainder of his life.<ref name="joseph" />

===Marriage===
Mustin married Frances Robina Woods in 1915. They remained married until Woods' death in 1969. The couple had no children.<ref name="joseph" />

==Death==
On January 28, 1977, Mustin died at Glendale Memorial Hospital in [[Glendale, California]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=vddBAAAAIBAJ&sjid=C6oMAAAAIBAJ&pg=5618,3247143&dq=burt+mustin+glendale&hl=en|title=Actor Mustin dies at 92|date=January 30, 1977|work=The Telegraph-Herald|page=2|accessdate=December 12, 2012}}</ref> He was buried in [[Forest Lawn Memorial Park (Hollywood Hills)|Forest Lawn - Hollywood Hills Cemetery]].
